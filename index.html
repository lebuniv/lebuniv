Social Network Model and Representation

Social Network Model

A social network model is a set of actors and the interactions between them. It is successfully represented as a graph, where actors are nodes and interactions are links. Actors can be connected by different kinds of ties and relations.

Key Concepts and Measures

  • Degree: Sum of connections (sum of the weights of connections in case of weighted graphs) from or to an actor.
  • Out-degree: Sum of connections from an actor to others.
  • In-degree: Sum of connections to an actor.
  • Closeness centrality: Distance of one actor to all others in the network.
  • Betweenness centrality: The number of shortest paths that pass through an actor, representing how frequently an actor is between other actors' geodesic paths.
  • Eigenvector: Measures the importance of an actor.
  • Cliques: Sub-set of actors more closely tied to each other than to actors who are not part of the subset.

Additional Network Metrics

  • Size: Number of nodes in the network.
  • HITS: A metric used in website analytics to measure hits or requests made to a website for file downloads.

Usage of Social Networks

Profile-based

Primarily organized around members' profile pages, containing information about an individual member, including their picture and details of interests, likes, and dislikes. Examples include Bebo, Facebook, and MySpace.

Content-based

In these services, user profiles play a secondary role to the posting of content. Examples include YouTube for video sharing, Last.fm for music monitoring and representation, and Flickr for photo sharing.

Micro-blogging

Allows users to publish short messages publicly or within contact groups. Examples include Twitter and Jaiku.

White-label

Offer some group-building functionality, allowing users to form mini-communities within sites. Examples include PeopleAggregator, Ning, and Colexn. However, they seem to be losing popularity.

Multi-user Virtual Environments

Environments that allow users to interact with each other's avatars. Examples include Second Life and World of Warcraft.

Mobile and Social Search

Mobile and social search services also contribute to the usage of social networks.

Additional Information

  • Social Relation: Anything that links two actors in a social network.
  • Social Network Analysis (SNA): Powerful analysis based on math/graph theory foundations.
  • Walk: A sequence of actors and relations that begins and ends with actors.
  • Geodesic Distance: The number of relations in the shortest possible walk from one actor to another.
  • Maximum Flow: The amount of different actors in the neighborhood of a source that lead to pathways to a target.
  • Social Networking vs. Social Networks: Social networking refers to the implementation of social networks in the digital world or media.
  • Social Roles: Defined by regularities in the patterns of relations among actors.
  • Density: The number of ties that are present compared to the total number of ties possible in a social network.
